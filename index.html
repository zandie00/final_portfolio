this e-Portfolio aims to present learning activities and instructional materials aligned with the Grade 3 Science competencies for Quarter 1 on the topic Matter. It aims to demonstrate how objects can be classified as solid, liquid, and gas based on observable properties, compile teaching artifacts that support interactive learning, and reflect on the process of designing effective science activities for Grade 3 learners.

MARRIEL CANARIA

Creating e-Portfolio for Grade 3 Science, specifically focusing on the topic of Matter, has been both a challenging and transformative experience. At the outset, I felt uncertain and overwhelmed. I did not know how to begin, what to include, or how to design the portfolio in a way that would truly reflect my teaching philosophy and instructional strategies. Initially, I thought an e-Portfolio was simply a collection of artifacts lesson plans, activities, and instructional materials. However, as I progressed, I realized that it is much more than that. It is a purposeful and creative representation of my identity as an educator, a mirror of my growth, and a tool for reflection. One of the first challenges I encountered was deciding on the platform and layout. With so many digital tools available, I struggled to choose one that would balance structure with creativity. Experimenting with different layouts taught me that presentation matters greatly. The way we organize and display our work influences how others perceive it. A well-designed portfolio can communicate clarity, professionalism, and creativity, while a poorly organized one may diminish the value of even the most thoughtful content. This realization pushed me to think critically about design choices, ensuring that my portfolio was not only functional but also visually engaging. The process of designing became an exercise in creativity. I had to merge technical skills with artistic expression, experimenting with colors, fonts, and layouts that would make the portfolio appealing without distracting from the content. This balance was not easy to achieve, but it allowed me to see how creativity and structure can coexist in educational work. It reminded me that teaching itself requires this balance lessons must be structured enough to meet learning objectives, yet flexible and creative enough to engage students meaningfully. Reflection was perhaps the most transformative part of this journey. As I compiled my teaching artifacts, I began to see patterns in my instructional style. I noticed how I consistently tried to integrate interactive and student-centered activities, such as group work, visual aids, and hands-on experiments. Reflection turned the portfolio into more than a showcase of materials; it became a mirror of my identity as an educator. I realized that my teaching is not just about delivering content but about fostering curiosity, creativity, and critical thinking in my students. This insight deepened my appreciation for the role of reflection in professional growth. It is through reflection that we connect our philosophy with our practice, identify areas for improvement, and celebrate our strengths. The project also reinforced the importance of thoughtful planning. At first, the idea of compiling my work into a digital portfolio felt overwhelming. However, breaking the process into smaller steps selecting artifacts, designing layouts, writing reflections helped me manage the task more effectively. This experience reminded me that planning is essential not only in creating portfolios but also in teaching. A well-planned lesson ensures that learning objectives are met, while thoughtful planning in professional tasks ensures clarity and coherence. Ultimately, this e-Portfolio journey has been a meaningful learning experience. It challenged me to step out of my comfort zone, to think critically about design and presentation, and to reflect deeply on my teaching practices. More than just a compilation of materials, the portfolio became a representation of my growth, creativity, and identity as an educator. It taught me that teaching is not only about knowledge delivery but also about how we present, reflect, and continuously improve. Moving forward, I see our group e-Portfolio not as a finished product but as a living document one that will evolve as I continue to grow in my profession and inspire my students to explore, question, and learn with curiosity and joy.

ALVIN JAY QUINITO
